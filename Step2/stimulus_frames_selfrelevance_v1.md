# Step 2 Stimulus Frames — Self-Relevance Theme (v1)
**Date:** 2026-05-27
**Theme:** Self-relevance — emphasizing how academic research benefits you personally, people you care about, and/or your local community
**Constraint:** Headlines and ledes may be edited; article body content is fixed
**Status:** Draft — under review by Nicole Cooper

---

## Overview

Four ASC news stories were selected as candidate stimuli. For each story, 3–4 headline/lede variants are provided, each foregrounding a different self-relevance angle:
- **Personal benefit** — research affects you directly
- **Relational benefit** — research affects people you care about
- **Community benefit** — research affects your local community

One story (con women/Hollywood) is flagged as a weak fit for this theme; see note below.

---

## Story 1: SCIMaP / Federal Funding Economic Impact

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/data-driven-interactive-map-shows-local-economic-impact-cuts-federal-funding-health-research

**Original headline:** Data-Driven, Interactive Map Shows Local Economic Impact of Cuts to Federal Funding for Health Research

**Original lede:** Researchers from the University of Pennsylvania and the University of Maryland have developed an interactive map called the Science & Community Impacts Mapping Project (SCIMaP) to visualize how proposed cuts to federal research funding would affect local economies. According to the team's analysis, reducing support for National Institutes of Health grants would result in an estimated $16 billion in economic losses and 68,000 job losses nationwide.

---

### Frame A — Local community (see your county)
**Headline:** See How Federal Research Cuts Would Hit Your Community

**Lede:** Proposed cuts to federal health research funding could mean real economic losses in your town — and now you can look it up. Researchers at Penn and the University of Maryland built an interactive map that shows the estimated job losses and economic impact in your county if NIH funding is reduced, part of a nationwide picture totaling $16 billion in losses and 68,000 jobs.

---

### Frame B — Personal + relational (jobs, health)
**Headline:** Federal Research Cuts Could Cost You and Your Neighbors Jobs. Penn Researchers Built a Map to Show You How.

**Lede:** Federal health research doesn't just happen in labs — it supports jobs, local businesses, and medical advances that protect you and the people you care about. Penn researchers have mapped exactly how proposed cuts to NIH funding would ripple through communities like yours, with losses estimated at $16 billion and 68,000 jobs nationwide.

---

### Frame C — Direct personal stakes
**Headline:** What Federal Research Cuts Mean for You, Your Job, and Your Community

**Lede:** Cuts to federal health research funding have a price tag — and part of it may be yours. A new Penn-led mapping project shows that proposed reductions to NIH support could cost the U.S. $16 billion in economic activity and 68,000 jobs, with impacts spread across counties nationwide, including your own.

---

### Frame D — Community economic identity
**Headline:** Penn Researchers Show the Local Price of Cutting Federal Health Research Funding

**Lede:** Federal investment in health research isn't an abstract line item — it pays for jobs and economic activity in communities across the country, including yours. A new interactive map from Penn and the University of Maryland lets you see exactly what's at stake in your county if proposed cuts to NIH funding go through.

## Story 2: Climate Change Action Strategies

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/what-are-most-effective-strategies-inspire-action-climate-change

**Original headline:** What Are the Most Effective Strategies To Inspire Action on Climate Change?

**Original lede:** Climate change presents a complex global challenge that often leaves individuals uncertain about meaningful action they can take. Researchers at the University of Pennsylvania's Communication Neuroscience Lab are conducting an intervention tournament to test six different strategies designed to shift beliefs and motivate behavioral responses related to climate change.

---

### Frame A — Personal empowerment
**Headline:** Penn Researchers Are Figuring Out How to Help You Take Action on Climate Change

**Lede:** If you've ever felt overwhelmed or unsure what you can actually do about climate change, Penn researchers are working on it. Scientists at the Communication Neuroscience Lab are testing six strategies designed to help people like you move from uncertainty to action — whether that means individual choices, community engagement, or supporting climate policy.

---

### Frame B — People you care about
**Headline:** Penn Research Aims to Help Protect the People You Love from the Effects of Climate Change

**Lede:** Climate change isn't an abstract global threat — it affects your family, your neighbors, and the places you love. Researchers at Penn's Communication Neuroscience Lab are running one of the largest studies of its kind to find out which strategies best motivate people to take action, with findings designed to help communities like yours respond.

---

### Frame C — Community action
**Headline:** New Penn Research Is Finding What Actually Motivates People in Communities Like Yours to Act on Climate

**Lede:** What does it take to get people to act on climate change — not just worry about it? Penn researchers are testing six different strategies with thousands of participants to find out, with a focus on motivating the kinds of individual, community, and policy actions that make a difference where you live.

---

### Frame D — Uncertainty + local relevance
**Headline:** Not Sure What You Can Do About Climate Change? Penn Researchers Are Finding Answers — for You and Your Community

**Lede:** Feeling uncertain about how to respond to climate change is common — and Penn scientists are studying exactly that. The Communication Neuroscience Lab is testing which strategies most effectively help people connect climate risks to their own lives and take meaningful action in their communities.

## Story 3: Sharing Trustworthy Information Online

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/how-get-people-share-trustworthy-information-online

**Original headline:** How To Get People To Share Trustworthy Information Online

**Original lede:** As social media becomes a primary news source, misinformation spreads rapidly online. Researchers at the Annenberg School for Communication investigated a novel approach: encouraging people to share high-quality, trustworthy information. Led by Danielle Cosme, the study examined what motivates sharing behavior across over 2,500 participants in the U.S. and Netherlands, focusing on climate change and health topics.

---

### Frame A — Personal protection (health/misinformation)
**Headline:** Penn Research Shows How Connecting News to Your Life Helps Fight Misinformation — for You and Your Family

**Lede:** Misinformation about health and climate can shape decisions you and the people you care about make every day. Penn researchers studied what gets people to share accurate information instead, and found a powerful lever: when people reflect on why a story matters to them personally or to their community, they're far more likely to pass it on.

---

### Frame B — Community empowerment
**Headline:** Want to Help Your Community Fight Misinformation? Penn Researchers Found a Simple Way

**Lede:** Misinformation spreads fast — but Penn researchers found that so can the truth. A study with over 2,500 people across two countries found that when people see how a news story connects to their own lives or their neighbors', they're significantly more likely to share it, helping accurate information reach more people in their communities.

---

### Frame C — You have power
**Headline:** You Have More Power Over Misinformation Than You Think. Here's What Penn Research Found.

**Lede:** Penn researchers discovered that one of the most effective defenses against misinformation isn't algorithms or fact-checkers — it's people like you. When individuals reflect on why accurate information matters to them or to people they care about, they're much more likely to share it, helping trustworthy content spread through their networks.

---

### Frame D — Relational/social
**Headline:** Your Sharing Decisions Shape What Your Community Believes. Penn Research Shows How to Use That for Good.

**Lede:** Every time you decide whether to share a news story, you're shaping what the people around you think and believe. Penn researchers studied the psychology of sharing across thousands of people and found that connecting news to personal and community relevance dramatically increases the spread of accurate information — with ripple effects that extend well beyond your own feed.

## Story 4: Why Hollywood Is Obsessed with Con Women

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/why-hollywood-so-obsessed-con-women

**Original headline:** Why is Hollywood So Obsessed with Con Women?

**Original lede:** Television shows featuring con women have recently proliferated on streaming platforms. Feminist scholars Sarah Banet-Weiser and Kathryn Claire Higgins examine this cultural trend in a new paper, arguing that shows like "Inventing Anna" and "The Dropout" reveal societal anxieties about gender, race, and capitalism in a post-#MeToo era.

> ⚠️ **Note:** This story is a weak fit for the self-relevance theme. The research is media criticism/feminist theory and does not connect naturally to how federal funding benefits people personally. The frames below require more editorial license than Stories 1–3. Consider substituting a different story for this slot, or reserving this story for a different frame type (e.g., researcher integrity, or knowledge/cultural value). Two frames are provided for reference.

---

### Frame A — Cultural relevance to your life
**Headline:** The Shows You're Watching Reflect Something Real About Your World. Penn Researchers Explain What.

**Lede:** From *Inventing Anna* to *The Dropout*, stories about women who deceive and defy expectations are everywhere — and Penn researchers argue that's no accident. Their new research shows these shows reflect real anxieties about gender, success, and power that shape the world you, your family, and the women in your community navigate every day.

---

### Frame B — Women in your community
**Headline:** Penn Researchers Explain Why Con Women Are Everywhere on TV — And What It Reveals About the World Your Daughters Inherit

**Lede:** The explosion of con women on streaming platforms isn't just a Hollywood trend — it reflects something deeper about how our society treats women who seek power and success. Penn scholars argue these shows reveal cultural tensions that affect women and girls in communities across the country, and that understanding them matters for the world we're building for the people we care about.
