## Frame Types

**Relevance frames** foreground that the research is *descriptively* connected to the participant's life, community, or people they care about. The participant is an affected party.

**Action frames** foreground that the participant can *do something* in response — look something up, share information, make choices, engage politically. The participant has agency.

## Story 1: SCIMaP / Federal Funding Economic Impact

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/data-driven-interactive-map-shows-local-economic-impact-cuts-federal-funding-health-research

**Original headline:** Data-Driven, Interactive Map Shows Local Economic Impact of Cuts to Federal Funding for Health Research

**Original lede:** Researchers from the University of Pennsylvania and the University of Maryland have developed an interactive map called the Science & Community Impacts Mapping Project (SCIMaP) to visualize how proposed cuts to federal research funding would affect local economies. According to the team's analysis, reducing support for National Institutes of Health grants would result in an estimated $16 billion in economic losses and 68,000 job losses nationwide.

---

### Relevance A
**Headline:** How Proposed Federal Research Cuts Would Affect Communities Like Yours, Mapped

**Lede:** Proposed cuts to federal health research funding won't play out in Washington alone — they'll hit counties like yours. Penn and University of Maryland researchers built an interactive map showing how reduced NIH funding would affect local economies, with projected losses of $16 billion and 68,000 jobs spread across communities nationwide, including your own.

---

### Relevance B
**Headline:** Federal Research Cuts Carry a Local Price Tag. Penn Researchers Show What's at Stake in Your County.

**Lede:** Federal investment in health research supports jobs and economic activity in communities across the country, including yours. A new mapping project from Penn and the University of Maryland shows the estimated economic loss and job cuts that would hit your county if proposed reductions to NIH funding go through.

---

### Action A
**Headline:** A New Penn Tool Lets You Look Up the Local Economic Impact of Federal Research Cuts

**Lede:** Penn and University of Maryland researchers built a tool that shows you the local stakes of federal health research funding. The Science & Community Impacts Mapping Project lets you enter your county and see the projected job and economic losses if NIH funding is cut — information you can use to understand the debate and engage with the people who make these decisions.

---

### Action B
**Headline:** Penn Researchers Built an Interactive Map Showing What Federal Research Cuts Would Mean for Your Community

**Lede:** Want to know what proposed cuts to federal health research funding would actually mean for your area? Penn and University of Maryland researchers built an interactive map so you can look it up. With $16 billion in economic losses and 68,000 jobs at stake nationwide, it gives you and your neighbors access to local evidence.

## Story 2: Climate Change Action Strategies

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/what-are-most-effective-strategies-inspire-action-climate-change

**Original headline:** What Are the Most Effective Strategies To Inspire Action on Climate Change?

**Original lede:** Climate change presents a complex global challenge that often leaves individuals uncertain about meaningful action they can take. Researchers at the University of Pennsylvania's Communication Neuroscience Lab are conducting an intervention tournament to test six different strategies designed to shift beliefs and motivate behavioral responses related to climate change.

---

### Relevance A
**Headline:** Penn Researchers Are Discovering What Helps People Like You Engage With Climate Change

**Lede:** Most people care about climate change and want to engage — they just aren't always sure how. Penn researchers at the Communication Neuroscience Lab are studying exactly that, testing six strategies to find out what best helps people like you connect climate issues to your own life and feel equipped to respond.

---

### Relevance B
**Headline:** Communities Like Yours Are at the Center of Penn's Largest-Ever Climate Engagement Study

**Lede:** Penn researchers are running one of the largest behavioral studies of its kind to understand what motivates people across communities like yours to engage with climate change. Rather than focusing on barriers, the research is identifying what actually works — with the goal of helping communities like yours build more effective, lasting responses.

---

### Action A
**Headline:** Penn Research Is Finding the Best Ways to Help You Act on Climate Change

**Lede:** Knowing that climate change is a problem isn't enough — Penn researchers are figuring out what actually helps people do something about it. Scientists at the Communication Neuroscience Lab are testing six strategies with thousands of participants to find the most effective ways to help people like you take action, whether through individual choices, community engagement, or policy advocacy.

---

### Action B
**Headline:** Not Sure What You Can Do About Climate Change? Penn Scientists Are Testing Six Approaches to Help People Like You

**Lede:** From individual behavior change to community and policy action, Penn researchers are systematically testing which strategies best equip people to respond to climate change. The findings are designed to give people — including you — clearer, more effective ways to engage with one of the defining challenges of our time.

## Story 3: Sharing Trustworthy Information Online

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/how-get-people-share-trustworthy-information-online

**Original headline:** How To Get People To Share Trustworthy Information Online

**Original lede:** As social media becomes a primary news source, misinformation spreads rapidly online. Researchers at the Annenberg School for Communication investigated a novel approach: encouraging people to share high-quality, trustworthy information. Led by Danielle Cosme, the study examined what motivates sharing behavior across over 2,500 participants in the U.S. and Netherlands, focusing on climate change and health topics.

---

### Relevance A
**Headline:** How Misinformation About Health and Climate Affects the Decisions People Like You Make

**Lede:** False and misleading information about health and climate isn't just an abstract problem — it shapes the choices people like you and those around you make every day. Penn researchers studied what gets people to share accurate information instead, finding that a simple act of reflection can shift what flows through your social networks.

---

### Relevance B
**Headline:** What You and Your Community Share Online Shapes What People Believe. Penn Research Reveals Why That Matters.

**Lede:** The information your community encounters online is shaped, in part, by the sharing decisions of people just like you. A Penn study with over 2,500 participants found that when people consider how a story relates to their own lives or their community, they're significantly more likely to pass on accurate information — and less likely to let misinformation fill the void.

---

### Action A
**Headline:** Penn Researchers Found That People Like You Have More Power Over Misinformation Than Expected

**Lede:** Penn researchers discovered that one of the most effective tools against misinformation is already in your hands. When people pause to reflect on why a news story matters to them or to people they care about, they become far more likely to share accurate information — turning individual awareness into a community-level defense against false content.

---

### Action B
**Headline:** Penn Research Found a Simple Way You Can Help Stop Misinformation in Your Community

**Lede:** Stopping the spread of misinformation doesn't require technology or expertise — it requires people like you making more deliberate sharing choices. Penn researchers found that when individuals reflect on how a news story connects to their own lives and communities, they're much more likely to share trustworthy content, with ripple effects that extend well beyond their own feed.

## Story 4A (Candidate): Climate Change Persuasion

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/news-climate-change-more-persuasive-expected-study-finds

**Original headline:** News on Climate Change Is More Persuasive Than Expected, Study Finds

**Original lede:** In a new paper, Computational Social Science Lab postdoctoral researcher Amir Tohidi and colleagues found that exposure to articles about climate change significantly increases climate change concerns among skeptics.

> **Note:** Core insight is that people underestimate how persuadable climate skeptics actually are. Clean fit for Relevance frames; Action frames are honest about the study's limits — attitude shifts were significant but did not translate to behavior or policy change (attitude-behavior gap).

---

### Relevance A
**Headline:** The Skeptics in Your Life May Be More Open to Climate Change Information Than You Think

**Lede:** If you've assumed the climate skeptics in your life are impossible to reach, Penn research suggests otherwise. A study of over 1,000 skeptics found that exposure to climate news articles produced meaningful shifts in concern — substantially more than most people predicted. The findings reveal a widespread tendency to underestimate how persuadable others actually are.

---

### Relevance B
**Headline:** Penn Research Reveals a Gap Between What People Expect About Climate Persuasion — and What Actually Happens

**Lede:** Most people assume climate skeptics are nearly impossible to persuade — but Penn research says otherwise. A study exposing over 1,000 skeptics to climate news found their concerns shifted meaningfully, far more than observers predicted. The findings have direct implications for anyone trying to have productive conversations about climate change with people they know.

---

### Action A
**Headline:** Penn Research Finds That Sharing Climate News With Skeptics Is More Effective Than Most People Expect

**Lede:** A new Penn study found that exposing climate skeptics to news articles significantly increased their climate concerns — more than most people would have predicted. If you've held back from sharing climate information with skeptical friends, family, or neighbors, the research suggests those conversations are more likely to shift views than most people would predict.

---

### Action B
**Headline:** Worried About Reaching the Climate Skeptics in Your Life? Penn Research Offers a Reason for Optimism

**Lede:** Penn researchers found that news articles about climate change shifted skeptics' views substantially — and that most people underestimated how much. For anyone navigating climate conversations with doubters in their family or community, the findings suggest well-framed climate reporting is more effective than expected.

## Story 4B (Candidate): mRNA Vaccine Mental Models

**URL:** https://www.asc.upenn.edu/news-events/news/mental-model-approach-shows-promise-reducing-susceptibility-misconceptions-about-mrna-vaccination

**Original headline:** 'Mental Model' Approach Shows Promise in Reducing Susceptibility to Misconceptions About mRNA Vaccination

**Original lede:** Researchers at the Annenberg Public Policy Center tested the effectiveness of a "mental model" approach to presenting scientific information, incorporating visual, verbal, or animated models to teach scientific or medical concepts to better identify misconceptions.

> **Note:** Strong fit for both frame types. Action frames are highly concrete — share a visual/animated explanation rather than just a rebuttal. Works whether encountered before or after misinformation exposure.

---

### Relevance A
**Headline:** Misinformation About mRNA Vaccines Is Spreading in Communities Like Yours — Penn Research Tested a Solution

**Lede:** Unfounded fears that mRNA vaccines could alter recipients' DNA have circulated widely — and may be shaping the health decisions of people you know. Penn researchers at the Annenberg Public Policy Center tested a new approach to correcting these misconceptions, with results that could help communities like yours respond more effectively to health misinformation.

---

### Relevance B
**Headline:** Misconceptions About mRNA Vaccines Are Common. Penn Research Found a Better Way to Address Them.

**Lede:** Misinformation about how mRNA vaccines work has reached communities across the country. Penn researchers found that sharing visual explanations of how the vaccines actually function is more effective at correcting misconceptions than conventional fact-checking — and it works whether people encounter it before or after seeing false claims.

---

### Action A
**Headline:** Want to Help Someone Understand mRNA Vaccines? Penn Research Found a Simple Approach That Works

**Lede:** If someone in your life has concerns about mRNA vaccines, Penn researchers found a more effective approach than simply pointing out what's false. Sharing a clear visual or animated explanation of how the vaccines actually work — a "mental model" — proved more effective at countering misinformation than fact-checking alone, whether people saw it before or after encountering false claims.

---

### Action B
**Headline:** Penn Research Found a Better Way to Counter Vaccine Misinformation. It's Simple Enough for Anyone to Use.

**Lede:** Sharing accurate information about mRNA vaccines works better when it explains the science visually. Penn researchers found that simple graphics and short animations showing how the vaccines work — rather than just correcting false claims — were more effective at reducing susceptibility to misinformation, and practical enough for anyone to use in conversation or on social media.
